# The sun has fallen down

I am not sure when it was; whether it was when I was born, when I took my first steps, spoke my first words, wrote my first poem; whether it was when I cried screams of childish terror at a 3 square inch portion of our mobile home carpet catching fire, or maybe it was when I lost my first friend; maybe it was when I stopped believing in divinity; maybe it was when I learned I was gay; maybe it was when I learned that my mother and I were (are) the same people, trapped in a strange land, when I learned she was unhappy in that small rural town with her children so far far away, her mother in the ground, and her father a continent apart; maybe it was when I, so young and na√Øve, learned that some children's favorite toy's were plastic bags not because they favored them but because there was nothing else to favor; maybe it was when the vice president called my people demons, or when the current president called us criminals; maybe it was when I broke things off with my first love, or maybe it was during the months after; maybe it was when I became homeless; maybe it was when I slept in a bathroom, cold tile, frosty floor; maybe it was yesterday; maybe I'm hoping it's tomorrow; maybe it is an innumerable and unpredictable number of years from now, and I am, in fact, a prophet. 

I really don't remember. I'm not quite sure when the sun fell down. 

Sometimes I see glimmers and rays, crepuscular, friendly, and tired (*solidified hope within my gaze*) of the fallen sun. Sometimes I think it will shoot back up into the sky. Sometimes I imagine that the sun is there, shining brighter than ever before, blinding me, us, with okayness. Sometimes I imagine that the great prophet, James Baldwin, will have his dream come true, and that when the sun does finally rise again, despite it being the last rise for many (*there will be a last rise for you and I as well*), we will nonetheless be unbound by our shackles: our totems and rituals, our taboos and blood sacrifices, our steeples, mosques, and synagogues, our races, armies, flags, nations, and families---our avoidance of death. Sometimes I imagine that bright blinding light from the sun---which has reclaimed its rightful spot in the sky is Baldwin's fire next time; that Baldwin's god has blinded us in fiery love.

But I look out and see the sun: not in the sky. Those radiant rays are not glimmers of light, but embers of the raging fire to come.

The fire next time will be from the fallen sun.
